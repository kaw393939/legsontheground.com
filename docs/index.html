Property & Housing

In Puerto Rico, a lot of deals never hit Zillow, Realtor, or even MLS. If you want off-market properties, you have to work the ground. Here’s how investors actually find them—and which methods work best:

  1. Walk the neighborhoods. Look for abandoned/overgrown homes, disconnected utilities, or mail piling up. Write down the address and parcel number (catastro), then pull owner info in CRIM and reach out.
  2. CRIM + Registro de la Propiedad. CRIM helps identify owners and tax status; the Registry confirms ownership, liens, and successions. Delinquent taxes and multiple heirs can be opportunities.
  3. Drive-for-dollars + local contacts. Neighbors often know the story: who owns it, whether the owner moved off-island, or if there’s a family dispute.
  4. Network with local pros. Attorneys (successions/divorce), notarios, property managers, contractors, electricians, plumbers—these people hear about deals first.
  5. Banks & cooperativas (in person). Many REO/foreclosure opportunities aren’t posted online. Ask for “Propiedades reposeídas.”
  6. Local auctions. Judicial auctions and bank postings can be great deals, but require title research and certified funds.
  7. Facebook Marketplace & WhatsApp groups. Search in Spanish (e.g., “casa abandonada,” “propiedad heredada,” “se vende por dueño”).
  8. Door knocking. Face-to-face often works better than cold calls in PR.
  9. Inherited properties (succession). Very common in PR—often discounted but can take longer to close.

Bottom line: Off-market in Puerto Rico = boots on the ground + local relationships.

Here’s a clear breakdown of the real costs so you can budget accurately (not just the purchase price):

1) Closing costs (buyer)

  • Notary fees: often ~1%–2% of purchase price (sales are notarized by attorney-notary).
  • Registry & government fees: filing + stamps/transfer fees vary by deal.
  • Title insurance (recommended): optional but wise for dispute protection.
  • Extra legal fees (if needed): depends on complexity.
  • Appraisal & inspection: typical add-ons that vary by property size/condition.
  • Lender fees (if financing): origination/underwriting/escrow, etc.

Typical total closing-cost budget: many buyers plan for ~3%–6% of purchase price (sometimes higher for complex transactions).

2) Ongoing costs after purchase

  • Property taxes (CRIM): often lower than mainland, but depends on classification and exemptions.
  • Insurance: hurricane exposure matters; flood insurance may be required in certain zones.
  • HOA/condo fees: if applicable.

Tip: Ask for a detailed closing-cost worksheet (“good faith estimate”) early so you’re not surprised at closing.

Not all flood zones are the same. Common FEMA zones you’ll see:

  • Zone X: low to moderate risk (insurance often optional).
  • Zone A / AE: higher risk (flood insurance required if financing).
  • Zone VE: very high risk (storm surge/waves) — usually not worth it unless you’re experienced and the numbers are strong.

When it can make sense: elevated property, no history of interior flooding (verify), reasonable insurance cost, and the price reflects the risk.

When to walk away: VE zones on the coast, visible water lines/mold patterns, neighbors say it floods often, or insurance kills the cash flow.

Due diligence checklist: confirm FEMA map, get insurance quotes before closing, ask neighbors about history, and verify drainage conditions and elevation when possible.

Yes — you can get a mortgage in Puerto Rico as a non-resident, but it’s more paperwork and varies by bank.

What lenders typically require

  • Valid ID (passport/driver’s license)
  • Proof of income and employment (tax returns, pay stubs, contracts)
  • Credit history (U.S. credit helps; some accept alternatives)
  • Proof of assets (bank/investment statements)
  • Higher down payment: commonly ~20–25%+ for non-residents

What to expect

  • Bank-by-bank variation (local lenders are often easier than mainland)
  • Stricter documentation and verification
  • Sometimes higher rates depending on profile

Tip: Get pre-approval early and work with a local mortgage broker and an attorney-notary familiar with PR closings.
